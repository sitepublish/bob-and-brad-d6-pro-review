Reactive Hyperemia — Reactive hyperemia is a transient increase in blood flow to a tissue or organ following a period of ischemia or mechanical stimulation. The phenomenon occurs because the local accumulation of vasodilatory metabolites, the release of nitric oxide from the endothelium, and the myogenic response of smooth muscle cells in arteriolar walls cause the blood vessels to dilate. In the context of percussion therapy, the rapid compression and release of muscle tissue creates intermittent changes in local pressure that stimulate reactive hyperemia. This increased blood flow delivers oxygen and nutrients while removing metabolic waste products such as lactic acid, contributing to the recovery benefits associated with massage gun use.

Scar Tissue Formation — Scar tissue forms as part of the body's natural healing response to injury, surgery, or inflammation, consisting primarily of type I collagen fibers laid down in a disorganized pattern compared to the parallel arrangement of normal tissue. While scar tissue serves the essential function of closing wounds and providing structural continuity, its irregular fiber arrangement can lead to reduced flexibility, restricted range of motion, and altered biomechanics in the affected area. As scar tissue matures over weeks and months, controlled mechanical loading and soft tissue mobilization can help remodel the collagen fibers into a more functional alignment. Percussion therapy devices can contribute to this remodeling process by delivering focused mechanical stimulation that helps break down excessive adhesions and promote more organized tissue repair.
