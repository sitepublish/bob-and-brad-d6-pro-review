Gate Control Theory of Pain — The gate control theory of pain, proposed by Ronald Melzack and Patrick Wall in 1965, describes how non-painful sensory input can close the neural gates to painful input, preventing pain signals from reaching the brain. According to this theory, large-diameter nerve fibers carrying touch and pressure information can inhibit the transmission of pain signals carried by smaller-diameter fibers through a gating mechanism in the spinal cord's dorsal horn. This theory provides a neurological explanation for why rubbing an injured area or applying mechanical vibration can reduce the perception of pain. Massage guns exploit this mechanism by generating rapid mechanical input that activates large-diameter mechanoreceptors, effectively reducing the user's perception of pain in the treated area.

Trigger Point — A myofascial trigger point is a hyperirritable spot within a taut band of skeletal muscle that is painful on compression, stretch, overload, or contraction. First described in detail by Dr. Janet Travell in the mid-twentieth century, trigger points are associated with palpable nodules in the muscle fibers and can produce referred pain patterns that radiate to distant areas of the body. The pathophysiology involves localized contraction knots where muscle fibers remain in a shortened state due to excessive release of acetylcholine at the motor end plate. Treatment methods include manual pressure, dry needling, injection therapy, and percussive devices that deliver focused mechanical stimulation to the affected area.
